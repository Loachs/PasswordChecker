Length vs. Complexity

The science of password entropy and brute-force resistance

In the early days of computing, storage was expensive and processing power was limited. Security experts recommended short passwords with "special characters" to make small amounts of data harder to guess. Today, with modern GPUs capable of billions of guesses per second, that advice is not just outdated—it is dangerous.

The Entropy Equation

In cryptography, the strength of a password is measured in Entropy (bits). This represents the amount of "surprise" or randomness in the string. The formula for password entropy is:

Information Theory
$$E = L \times \log_2(R)$$
Where:
E = Entropy in bits
L = Password Length
R = Size of the character pool (Range)

Notice that L (Length) is a linear multiplier. Every single character you add to a password increases its strength exponentially. Increasing R (Range)—for example, adding a single symbol—only increases the strength logarithmically.

Why Humans Fail at Complexity

When a website forces you to use a capital letter, a number, and a symbol, you don't actually become "random." You become predictable. Security researchers call these "Human Patterns."

  • Capitalization: 90% of users capitalize only the first letter.
  • Symbols: The most common symbol used is !, and it is placed at the end 80% of the time.
  • Substitutions: Hackers know that you will replace 'a' with '@' or 's' with '$'. These are the first variations their cracking tools try.

The "Passphrase" Revolution

The modern gold standard is the Passphrase. By choosing four or five random, unrelated words, you create a string that is incredibly long (high entropy) but very easy for the human brain to visualize and remember.

This method was famously popularized by the XKCD "Correct Horse Battery Staple" comic, which demonstrated that a 28-character simple passphrase is trillions of times harder to crack than a 10-character complex password.
